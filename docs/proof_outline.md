# Proof Outline

The formalization follows the standard-bitableau route to the Grobner-basis
theorem for determinantal ideals.

## 1. Generic Minors

The basic algebraic layer defines ordered row and column choices for minors and
interprets each choice as the determinant of a submatrix of the generic matrix
`Matrix.mvPolynomialX`. This gives the generic minor polynomials and the
determinantal ideals generated by all minors of a fixed size.

## 2. Leading Terms

The term-order layer defines diagonal and anti-diagonal term-order predicates.
For the concrete anti-diagonal lexicographic order, the anti-diagonal
permutation term of every generic minor is proved to be the leading term.

## 3. Young Bitableaux and Width

Young bitableaux encode products of minors. Their length is the number of minor
factors, and their degree is the total polynomial degree. Generalized
permutations and monomial exponent vectors are equipped with a width statistic
that matches the length statistic on standard bitableaux.

## 4. KRS Correspondence

The KRS layer implements forward row insertion and reverse row insertion, then
packages them as an equivalence

```lean
KRS.krsEquiv m n :
  ((Fin m × Fin n) →₀ ℕ) ≃ StandardYoungBitableau m n
```

The key consequences are degree preservation and the equality between monomial
width and standard-bitableau length.

## 5. Straightening

The straightening layer proves that any bitableau polynomial can be rewritten as
a finite linear combination of standard-bitableau polynomials. The expansion
preserves degree and respects the length filtration. This provides the
standard-bitableau spanning statements and the linear independence needed in
the determinantal quotient.

## 6. Standard-Bitableau Basis

For the quotient ring by `J_r`, the standard bitableaux of length at most `r`
form a basis. Larger minors vanish in the quotient, while straightening reduces
arbitrary bitableaux to standard ones.

## 7. Hilbert-Function Comparison

The KRS correspondence identifies the degree-`d` basis elements of the
determinantal quotient with monomial exponent vectors of degree `d` and width
at most `r`. The same count is compared with the quotient by the anti-diagonal
initial ideal.

## 8. Grobner-Basis Conclusion

If the initial ideal generated by the leading terms of `G_{r+1}` were strictly
smaller than the true initial ideal of `J_r`, the Hilbert functions of the two
quotients would differ in some degree. The width count and the
standard-bitableau basis rule out this strict inequality, proving that
`G_{r+1}` is a Grobner basis.
